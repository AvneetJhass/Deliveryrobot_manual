Individual Build Manual
Build Your Delivery Robot Parts
Your personal homework build guide research it, plan it, build it.
What is this manual for? In your next design class you will assemble your delivery robot together with your group. Before that class, your job is to build your assigned part at home. This manual tells you exactly what to make, what size, what materials to use, and how to prepare for class.

You will NOT assemble the robot at home. Build your part, label it, bag it. Assembly happens in class.
Before you start The ONE Rule

Every part you make must connect to your problem statement.

Open your Worksheet 1 right now to your problem statement. Before you make every single part, ask yourself: How does this part help the users in my specific deployment area?

If you cannot answer that stop. Go back to your sketch. Redesign the part so it actually solves the real problem.

Did you know? Real delivery robots like Starship went through over 100 design versions before working on real pavements. Hospital robots like TUG navigated 40 different corridor layouts before deployment. Your first version does not need to be perfect. It just needs to exist and connect to your user's problem.

Step 2 Materials What You Can Use

You can use anything you find at home. Here are ideas but you are not limited to this list. The best prototypes use creative substitutions.

Structure and Body for the main chassis and body panels
Corrugated cardboard (delivery boxes) Cardboard tubes (toilet roll, kitchen roll) Plastic bottles (330ml or 500ml) Plastic boxes or containers (food storage, lunch boxes) Foam board Shoebox or small box Thick paper / newspaper rolls Ice cream sticks / lolly sticks
Joining and Strengthening
Masking tape Clear or packing tape PVA or white craft glue Hot glue gun String or rubber bands Velcro strips Brass paper fasteners
Sensors, Features and Cargo
Ping pong balls (sensor domes) LED fairy lights Small mirror or kitchen foil (camera lens) Pipe cleaners Foam offcuts (cargo lining) Plastic or zip-lock bag (waterproof compartment) Small hinge (opening door or flap) Buttons from old toys (audio or alert button) Bottle caps painted black (camera)
Decorative and Expressive to show design intent
Paint (acrylic or watercolour) Coloured paper or cardstock Stickers or washi tape Markers and pens Felt or fabric scraps Tissue paper Glitter (use sparingly!)
Area-Specific Ideas depending on your deployment area
Miniature traffic cones (city streets) Red and green card circles (traffic signals) Red cross symbol (hospital) Room number labels (hotel) School logo sticker (school campus) Intercom button (residential society) Ramp or slope piece (accessibility)
Remember: If you cannot find the exact material substitute. A milk bottle cap works like a bought wheel. Newspaper rolled into a tube works like a cardboard tube. The material does not need to be perfect. The idea behind it does.

Step 4 Build Your Parts Part by Part Guide

Use these guides for each part. You only need the parts that are in your sketch and your assigned role.

A
The Robot Body (Main Chassis)
Build this first everything attaches to it
What it is
The central box or cylinder that holds all other parts. This is the first thing anyone in your deployment area sees when the robot approaches.
Max size
Max 25 cm tall x 20 cm wide x 20 cm deep. Must leave room for wheels below and feature on top or side.
Material ideas
Shoebox (easiest). Plastic storage box. Cardboard folded into a box. Plastic bottle with label removed. Foam board panels.
How to make it
If using a shoebox: cover with plain card. If folding card: score fold lines with a ruler and blunt pencil first. Reinforce inside corners with small cardboard triangles.
C
Cargo Compartment
Where food, packages, medicine, or items are stored
What it is
The section where deliveries are stored. Depending on your deployment area, this might need to open easily, keep things cold, be sterile, or be easy to unlock without bending down.
Max size
Max 15 x 15 x 15 cm. Must fit inside or on top of the body.
Material ideas
Small box inside the main body. Zip-lock bag glued inside. Foam lined container. Divided compartment using cardboard walls inside a plastic box.
How to make it
Cut a rectangular flap in the front of the body and fold it forward to make a door. Use masking tape as a hinge. Line the inside with foam if designing for fragile or medical deliveries.
D
Your User-Specific Feature
The most important part it directly solves your problem statement
This part makes your robot different. It directly addresses the specific users in your deployment area. Below are examples by area but design YOUR feature from YOUR problem statement, not from this list.
Mall
Wide base for stability in crowds. Child-safe panels with no sharp edges. Screen at standing adult height.
Hotel
Silent operation indicator. Multilingual screen panel. Discreet colouring to blend with hotel interiors.
School Campus
Fun or friendly exterior. Locked compartment students cannot open without a code. Loud alert signal for noisy corridors.
Residential Society
Intercom buzzer. Height-adjustable hatch for elderly. Rain cover for outdoor use.
Hospital
Hatch at bed-reach height (30 cm from floor). No touch door sensor. Sterile white finish. Red cross marking.
City Streets
Flashing light at pedestrian crossing. Wide wheels for kerbs and uneven pavements. Weatherproof cover.
E
Sensors and Safety Indicators
Optional but recommended they do not need to work
What it is
Small elements representing cameras, distance sensors, or warning lights. They represent the function, not perform it.
Max size
Each sensor element: max 4 cm diameter. Place at front, back, or sides.
Material ideas
Ping pong ball halves. Foil circles. Button cells from old toys. LED fairy lights. Coloured stickers. Bottle caps painted black.
How to make it
Attach sensor domes to the front face using tape or glue. Label each one: "Distance sensor", "Camera", "Obstacle detector". If using lights, tape the battery pack inside and thread the wire to the sensor position.
Step 5 Make Your Parts Strong

A delivery robot that collapses in a hospital corridor or falls apart in a school hallway is useless. Use at least two of the three techniques below.

TechniqueHow to do it and where to use it
Roll a strip of cardboard tightly around a pencil. Tape the edge. Slide out the pencil. Press down much stronger than flat card. Use for legs, support columns, and the robot body frame.
Fold a strip of cardboard at 90 degrees (score the fold line with a ruler first). The L-shape resists bending. Use for wall panels, doorframes, and hatch edges.
Cut a small right-angle triangle from cardboard and glue it into any corner where two panels meet. Triangles cannot be squashed sideways. Use at every corner of the robot body.
Watch outDo NOT use tape as the only thing holding a joint together. Tape stretches under weight. Use tape to hold pieces in position while glue dries then glue becomes the actual joint.

Step 7 Label and Final Check Before You Bag

This is the step most students skip. Do not skip it. In class your teacher will check each part against your sketch. Unlabelled parts waste everyone's time.

1

Tear off a small piece of masking tape and stick it onto each part.

2

Write the part name in pencil (e.g. "Front wheel left side").

3

Below that, write the design influence it addresses (e.g. "Inclusivity" or "Safety").

4

On your FEATURE part, also write: "This helps [specific user] in [your area] because…" in 7 words or less.
